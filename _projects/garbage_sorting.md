An interactive web documentary that makes waste sorting easier to understand through animation, visual storytelling, and playful interaction. The project turns dry public information into a more engaging learning experience for citizens in Odense.

![Webdoc overview]({{ '/assets/resources/waste-sorting-webdoc.png' | relative_url }})

## The idea

The project focused on waste sorting in Odense and explored how public information could be made more engaging than a traditional brochure.

Instead of only presenting text, the idea was to create a webdoc — an interactive web documentary — where users could scroll, click, and learn through graphics, animation, and small interactive elements.

The goal was to help users understand what to sort, why sorting matters, and how different types of waste should be handled. The project built on Odense Renovation’s existing sorting brochure, but aimed to make the information more visual, easier to follow, and more memorable.

![Odense visual identity]({{ '/assets/resources/waste-sorting-odense.png' | relative_url }})

## Target

The target audience was citizens in Odense who need to sort household waste but may find the existing information too text-heavy or hard to engage with.

The project was especially aimed at users who want quick, practical guidance: what belongs in each bin, why it matters, and what happens to the waste after collection.

The local Odense setting was important because waste sorting systems can vary between municipalities.

![Food waste section]({{ '/assets/resources/waste-sorting-food.png' | relative_url }})

## The process

The project was developed through an iterative design process. It began with brainstorming, sketches, and storyboarding before moving into low-fidelity paper prototypes and later a high-fidelity digital prototype.

The webdoc was built using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, while the mini-game was created in Unity with C#. Visual assets and animations were produced with Adobe Illustrator, After Effects, LottieFiles, and Lottie Interactivity.

The structure was built around three key questions: what, why, and how. This helped organise the information so users first understood the waste type, then why it should be sorted, and finally how to sort it correctly.

Several tests and feedback rounds were conducted. Early prototypes were tested with fellow students, who helped confirm the information flow and asked for more practical sorting guidance.

Later feedback pointed out that the trash-bin navigation took up too much space, the background made some text hard to read, and the project needed a stronger Odense atmosphere. This led to design changes such as using the bins as navigation, improving readability, adding a clearer local background, and focusing the final prototype on food waste as a vertical slice.

The final webdoc included scroll-based animations, interactive flip cards with extra information, a progress truck, and a small sorting game where users could test their knowledge.

The main learning from the project was the value of narrowing the scope earlier, testing more often, and using visuals and interaction to make educational information easier to understand.
