## The idea

The King's Advisor is an interactive narrative game inspired by fantasy, political intrigue, chamber drama, and choice-based games such as Papers, Please, Blind Justice, Lapse, and Until Dawn.

The player takes the role of the king’s advisor and nephew, who must help manage a kingdom in conflict. The core idea was to create a story where every choice has consequences, and where the player is pulled between loyalty to the king and sympathy for the people suffering under his rule.

The game was designed as a graphic novel with branching dialogue, multiple possible outcomes, and a strong focus on moral decision-making.

## Target

The project was aimed at players who enjoy dramatic stories, fantasy worlds, political tension, and replaying narrative games to explore “what if” scenarios.

The experience was designed for players who want to take part in the story rather than only read it. By giving the player control over important decisions, the game encourages reflection on power, morality, and consequence.

## The process

The project was developed by a team of five students with different roles across writing, programming, visual design, music, and project organisation.

The process began with brainstorming and paper prototypes of branching story structures. Early versions included several resource systems, such as economy, military, population happiness, and external relations. Later in the process, the system was simplified so the focus stayed on the story and the player’s moral direction.

The narrative was first mapped and tested in Twine before being implemented in Ren'Py, which was chosen because it fit the graphic novel format better than Unity. This made it easier to work with dialogue, character portraits, choices, and branching scenes.

The visual style was developed through a pixel art process. The team collected references, created early character drafts, adjusted outlines and facial details, and gradually built a consistent visual identity for the characters, backgrounds, and UI. Music was also created for the different factions to strengthen the worldbuilding and give each culture a distinct atmosphere.

Testing was mostly done internally and with classmates, friends, and family. The feedback helped identify bugs, improve dialogue flow, and polish the first act of the story. The final result was a functional first act of a larger narrative game, with original writing, artwork, music, and branching player choices.
