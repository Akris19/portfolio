## The idea

Treasure Hunt started as a co-op puzzle concept where two players would solve challenges together. During development, the idea changed direction and became a competitive treasure hunt game set inside a pyramid.

The goal was to create a simple but tense game experience where players compete against each other while also dealing with danger from the environment. Each player has a torch, must navigate a maze, collect treasures, and avoid being scared by mummies.

The final concept focused on fast rounds, simple controls, and a clear objective: collect enough treasure and get out before the time runs out.

## Target

The project was aimed at players who enjoy local multiplayer, simple competitive games, maze navigation, and quick arcade-like challenges.

The design focused on being easy to understand and quick to play. The controls were kept simple, using WASD for one player and the arrow keys for the other, so two players could play on the same keyboard without needing extra equipment.

## The process

The project went through several design changes. The earliest version was a cooperative puzzle game, but after prototyping and discussion, the team moved toward a more focused versus game with treasure hunting, mummies, and a pyramid maze.

The map design changed many times throughout the process. Early prototypes were made on paper to test puzzle ideas, maze layouts, and the placement of key objects. Later versions were implemented in Unity, where the team refined the labyrinth, lighting, player movement, treasure placement, and mummy behaviour.

The main mechanics included two players, limited torchlight, collectible treasures, and mummies that patrol the maze. If a player gets too close to a mummy, they are scared and sent back to their patrol point, which adds risk and pressure while exploring.

A large part of the process focused on polish and game feel. Animations, screen shake, sound effects, music, and visual feedback were used to make the game feel more responsive and satisfying. Playtesting was done several times with classmates, friends, and family, and the feedback was used to improve the game’s clarity, pacing, and overall experience.

The main learning from the project was the value of prototyping early, changing direction when needed, and using playtesting to decide which mechanics should stay, be improved, or be removed.
